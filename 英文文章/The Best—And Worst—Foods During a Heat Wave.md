

Summer has always been a two-headed beast. It's the season of hiking, biking, swimming, and afternoons at the beach. Then too, it is the season of heat waves—sweating through your lightest clothes, of climbing into a blast-furnace car, and of triple-digit temperatures leaving you spent, wilted, irritable, and unmotivated. Practically every aspect of your life is affected when thermometers pop their tops—including your diet.

What you eat is intimately entwined with day-to-day temperatures, so it pays to know what food choices to make.

Hot weather is a known appetite-suppressant, with the body seeking to thermoregulate by limiting the excess heat generated by digestion. The brain's hypothalamus has heat-sensing proteins that are generated when temperatures rise, creating the sensation of satiety and fullness even if you haven't eaten. But your need for nutrition is great when it's hot out as it is when it's cold. The key is picking the right things to eat—foods that replenish fluids, electrolytes, and other nutrients that a heat wave takes away from you.

A good start in determining what to eat in the heat is knowing what not to eat. Heavy, greasy foods are a general no-go. That fried chicken picnic lunch or grilled ribs cookout may seem appealing, but such fat- and calorie-dense foods can be harder to digest, raising body temperature and leaving you feeling overheated and sluggish. Similarly, salty processed foods such as chips and cured meats can worsen dehydration by increasing sodium intake, especially if you're not drinking enough fluids.

Two hot weather staples—cold beer and iced tea—may also be doing your body no favors. Alcohol and caffeine are diuretics, stimulating the urinary system and causing you to excrete water at the very time you need it the most. Full-fat dairy and high sugar drinks can cause other problems. The dairy slows down gastric transit, increasing body temperature as a result. Sugar, meantime, messes with hydration because it pulls fluids into the gut. You're better off with flavored water or homemade fresh fruit pops.

The foods you should include in your diet are a lot more numerous than the ones you should exclude. In place of heavy grilled meats, go for lean, cold protein, including hard-boiled eggs, tuna salad, and cold shrimp salad. Legume-based salads can also be high in protein and easy to digest. Do remember that such foods are very perishable and need to be handled properly in the heat. Hot weather increases the risk of food-borne illnesses, so keep cold foods below 40°F and don't leave perishable items out for more than one hour.

Even more important than what you're eating is what you're drinking. Your body loses water fast in the heat, and replacing it all takes some doing. Sipping fluids throughout the day is a good starting point, and checking your urine color can help determine if you're becoming dehydrated. It's not just water that's lost to the heat; it's electrolytes too. These minerals help regulate a host of bodily operations, including nerve and muscle function. For a quicker hit, you can turn to sports drinks, coconut water, or tart cherry juice.

Fruits and vegetables are rich sources of both water and electrolytes. The average piece of produce is 80% water, with watermelon topping out at 90% and cucumbers at an almost 97% apiece. Smoothies can be a good way to get the hydration and electrolyte benefits of fruit in a cold and sweet treat that spares you the heavy sugar and dairy hit of a milkshake. When it comes to regulating body temperature, some people find that spicy foods can actually be beneficial, since they stimulate sweating, which ultimately cools you down. But it's best to make sure you're fully hydrated before tucking into fiery wings, as spicy foods can lead to stomach irritation.

Summertime picnics and barbecues have always been part of the joy of the season. Navigating the culinary landscape of a heat wave requires a delicate balance of hydration and smart nutritional choices. By prioritizing restorative fare and remaining mindful of your body's needs, you can savor the delights of the season without compromising your well-being, proving that choosing fun and satisfying foods need not be separated from choosing smart and healthy ones.
